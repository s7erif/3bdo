Tuberculosis

Comprehensive Pediatric Review

Overview

Tuberculosis (TB) is a chronic infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It is transmitted person to person via droplet infection and remains prevalent in areas with poor nutrition, overcrowding, and limited healthcare.

Natural History & Transmission

  • Inhalation of droplet nuclei containing bacilli.
  • Primary infection in lungs with lymph node involvement.
  • May heal, remain latent, or progress to disease.
  • Risk higher in infants, malnourished and immunocompromised children.

Primary Pulmonary TB

  • Primary complex: small lung focus + large regional lymphadenitis.
  • Hilar adenopathy is characteristic.
  • May cause bronchial compression → hyperinflation or atelectasis.
  • Often resolves with treatment, may leave calcification.

Clinical Features

  • Non-productive cough, mild dyspnea.
  • Fever, night sweats.
  • Anorexia, weight loss, failure to thrive.
  • Many children may have minimal physical findings.

Progressive Pulmonary Disease

  • Enlarging caseous focus with cavitation.
  • High fever, productive cough.
  • Weight loss and night sweats.
  • Signs: crepitations, dullness over cavity.

Pleural Effusion

  • Due to spread from subpleural focus or lymph nodes.
  • Symptoms: fever, chest pain, dyspnea.
  • Usually unilateral, exudative fluid.
  • Excellent prognosis with therapy.

TB Pericarditis

  • Rare; spread from mediastinal lymph nodes.
  • Features: fever, distant heart sounds, pulsus paradoxus.
  • May progress to constrictive pericarditis.
  • May require pericardiectomy.

Miliary TB

  • Hematogenous dissemination to multiple organs.
  • Common in infants and immunocompromised.
  • Features: respiratory distress, hepatosplenomegaly, meningitis.
  • Chest X-ray: diffuse millet-sized nodules.

Lymph Node TB

  • Most common extrapulmonary TB in children.
  • Firm, non-tender, matted nodes.
  • May caseate and form sinus tracts.
  • Diagnosis by histopathology/AFB.

TB Meningitis

  • Stage 1: fever, headache, irritability.
  • Stage 2: neck stiffness, seizures, cranial nerve palsies.
  • Stage 3: coma, paralysis, death.
  • High mortality without early treatment.

Abdominal & Perinatal TB

  • Peritonitis: abdominal pain, ascites.
  • Enteritis: diarrhea, pain, weight loss.
  • Congenital TB: respiratory distress, hepatosplenomegaly, FTT.
  • Often associated with maternal TB.

Diagnosis

  • History of contact with TB case.
  • Chest X-ray.
  • AFB stain & culture (gastric aspirate/sputum).
  • GeneXpert where available.
  • CSF analysis for meningitis.

Tuberculin Skin Test (Mantoux)

  • Intradermal PPD, read after 48–72 hours.
  • Positive: ≥10 mm induration.
  • BCG may cause small reactions but >10 mm suggests infection.
  • False negatives: malnutrition, young age, immunosuppression.

Anti-TB Drugs

  • Isoniazid (H), Rifampicin (R)
  • Pyrazinamide (Z), Ethambutol (E)
  • Streptomycin in selected cases.
  • Ethionamide for drug-resistant TB.

Standard Treatment Regimen

  • Intensive phase: 2 months HRZE.
  • Continuation phase: 4 months HR.
  • Total duration: usually 6 months.
  • Longer for meningitis, bone TB.

Corticosteroids

  • Indicated in TB meningitis.
  • Miliary TB.
  • Pleural or pericardial effusion.
  • Endobronchial TB.

Prevention

  • Early detection and treatment of cases.
  • Contact screening.
  • Isoniazid prophylaxis for exposed children.
  • BCG vaccination at birth.

Prognosis

  • Excellent with early diagnosis and compliance.
  • Delayed treatment → high morbidity & mortality.
  • TB meningitis and miliary TB have worse outcomes.
