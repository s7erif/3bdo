Viral Infections in Children

Exanthematous & Systemic Viral Diseases

Overview

Pediatric viral infections are common causes of fever and rash in children. They are usually self-limited but may cause serious complications, especially in infants and immunocompromised patients.

Common Viral Exanthems

  • Measles (Rubeola)
  • Rubella (German measles)
  • Varicella (Chickenpox)
  • Mumps
  • Infectious mononucleosis (EBV)
  • Erythema infectiosum (Parvovirus B19)
  • Roseola infantum (HHV-6/7)

Measles

  • Highly contagious RNA virus.
  • Prodrome: fever, cough, coryza, conjunctivitis.
  • Koplik spots on buccal mucosa.
  • Maculopapular rash starts at face → spreads downward.
  • Complications: pneumonia, otitis media, encephalitis.
  • Management: supportive + vitamin A.

Rubella

  • Mild illness with low-grade fever.
  • Posterior auricular & occipital lymphadenopathy.
  • Rash spreads rapidly and fades within 3 days.
  • Major risk: congenital rubella syndrome.
  • Prevention: MMR vaccination.

Varicella (Chickenpox)

  • Vesicular rash in crops: macule → papule → vesicle → crust.
  • Pleomorphic rash is characteristic.
  • Starts on trunk and face.
  • Complications: secondary infection, pneumonia, encephalitis.
  • Treatment: supportive ± acyclovir in high-risk.

Mumps

  • Painful parotid gland swelling.
  • Fever, malaise.
  • Complications: orchitis, meningitis, pancreatitis, deafness.
  • Treatment: supportive.
  • Prevention: MMR vaccine.

Infectious Mononucleosis (EBV)

  • Fever, sore throat, fatigue.
  • Generalized lymphadenopathy.
  • Hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Atypical lymphocytosis.
  • Avoid sports → risk of splenic rupture.

Erythema Infectiosum (Parvovirus B19)

  • "Slapped cheek" rash.
  • Lacy rash on trunk and limbs.
  • Mild illness, usually afebrile.
  • Risk: aplastic crisis in hemolytic anemia.
  • Supportive treatment.

Roseola Infantum

  • High fever for 3–5 days → sudden fall.
  • Then maculopapular rash appears.
  • Common in infants <2 years.
  • Complication: febrile convulsions.
  • Treatment: antipyretics.

Diagnosis

  • Usually clinical based on rash pattern.
  • Serology or PCR if needed.
  • CBC: lymphocytosis in many viral infections.
  • Specific tests for EBV, rubella, measles if suspected.

Management Principles

  • Supportive care: fluids, antipyretics.
  • Avoid aspirin (risk of Reye syndrome).
  • Antivirals in selected cases (acyclovir).
  • Treat complications.
  • Isolation for contagious cases.

Prevention

  • Routine immunization (MMR, varicella).
  • Hand hygiene & infection control.
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis if indicated.
