Morphine (Opiates & Opioids)

Complete Pharmacological and Toxicological Summary

Comprehensive information on opiates, opioids, their mechanisms, clinical effects, and management of toxicity.

Opiates vs Opioids

  • Opiates: Natural alkaloids from opium
  • Opioids: Synthetic drugs with morphine-like action

Source

  • Opium poppy (Papaver somniferum)
  • Contains ~20 compounds
  • Morphine (10%), codeine (0.5%), papaverine, thebaine
  • 1000 g opium → 100 g morphine → 10 g heroin

Pharmacokinetics

  • Well absorbed orally & parenterally
  • Large volume of distribution (>5 L/kg)
  • Metabolized mainly in liver (conjugation)
  • Excretion: kidneys (detectable metabolites)
  • Also excreted in milk, bile, saliva, stomach

Mechanism of Action

Acts on opioid receptors:

  • Mu: supraspinal analgesia, respiratory depression, euphoria, dependence
  • Delta: modulates Mu receptor action
  • Kappa: spinal analgesia, sedation, miosis
  • Sigma: vasomotor/respiratory stimulation, dysphoria

Effects:

  • CNS: analgesia, sedation, euphoria, respiratory depression
  • GIT: ↓ motility, ↑ sphincter tone → constipation
  • Urinary tract: ↓ motility, ↑ sphincter tone → retention

Therapeutic Uses

  • Severe pain (MI, renal colic)
  • Acute pulmonary edema (sedative, ↓ resistance)
  • Treatment of opioid addiction
  • Codeine (anti-cough), diphenoxylate (anti-diarrheal)
  • Regional anesthesia, CVS surgery

Toxicology

  • Mode: Accidental, rarely suicidal/homicidal
  • Fatal dose: 2 g opium, 100 mg morphine (↑ tolerance in addicts)
  • Fatal period: 6–12 h
  • Cause of death: respiratory depression

Clinical Picture

CNS:

  • Early euphoria → dysphoria
  • Restlessness → drowsiness, coma

Respiration:

  • Slow stertorous breathing
  • Later Cheyne–Stokes, apnea

CVS:

  • Slow full pulse
  • Circulatory collapse

Other:

  • Hypothermia, vomiting, cyanosis
  • Pinpoint pupils (miosis)

Terminal:

  • Dilated fixed pupils
  • Death from respiratory failure

Morphine vs Pontine Hemorrhage
Investigations

Differential Diagnosis of Pinpoint Pupils

  • Organophosphorus poisoning
  • Phenothiazines
  • Pontine hemorrhage

Morphine Poisoning:

  • Poisoning history
  • Hypothermia
  • Normal BP
  • Sluggish reflexes
  • No paralysis
  • Naloxone positive

Pontine Hemorrhage:

  • Head trauma/HTN
  • Hyperthermia
  • Hypertension
  • Exaggerated reflexes
  • Quadriplegia
  • No naloxone effect

Investigations

  • Routine: ABG, electrolytes
  • Specific: drug levels in blood/urine (detectable 3 days)
  • Chest X-ray: pulmonary edema
  • ECG: arrhythmias from hypoxia

Treatment

  • Emergency: ABC (Airway, Breathing, Circulation)
  • Decontamination: emesis, gastric lavage (even IV), MDAC, WBI (body packers)
  • Specific antidotes:
    • Naloxone: DOC, 0.1–0.4 mg IV, repeatable, duration 1–4 h, routes IM/SC/ET
    • Naltrexone: oral, 50 mg/day, long action (36–48 h), used in chronic cases
    • Nalmefene: 4x potency of naloxone, longer half-life, 0.25–1 mg IV
    • Agonist–antagonists (Nalorphine): obsolete
  • Supportive: coma care, warming, treat shock
  • No effect in normal individuals, causes withdrawal in addicts, dramatic response in intoxication

Key Points to Remember

  • Morphine acts on multiple opioid receptors with different effects
  • Respiratory depression is the primary cause of death in overdose
  • Pinpoint pupils are characteristic but not pathognomonic
  • Naloxone is the specific antidote with rapid reversal of effects
  • Tolerance develops in addicts, requiring higher fatal doses
