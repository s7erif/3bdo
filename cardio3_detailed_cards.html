Heart Murmurs & Pediatric Arrhythmias

Overview

Cardio 3 focuses on the evaluation of heart murmurs in children and common pediatric arrhythmias, their causes, clinical presentation, investigations, and management.

Heart Murmur

A heart murmur is a sound produced by turbulent blood flow within the heart or great vessels, heard during cardiac auscultation.

  • May be innocent (physiologic) or pathological.
  • Very common in childhood.

Innocent (Functional) Murmurs

  • No structural heart disease.
  • Child is asymptomatic.
  • Soft (grade ≤2), systolic, short.
  • Vary with position or activity.
  • Examples: Still’s murmur, pulmonary flow murmur.

Pathological Murmurs

  • Associated with symptoms: cyanosis, dyspnea, failure to thrive.
  • Loud (grade ≥3), harsh, pansystolic or diastolic.
  • Associated abnormal heart sounds.
  • Often due to CHD (VSD, PDA, AS).

Red Flags in Murmurs

  • Diastolic murmur.
  • Holosystolic murmur.
  • Grade ≥3/6.
  • Cyanosis or heart failure.
  • Abnormal pulses or blood pressure.

Evaluation of a Murmur

  • Detailed history: symptoms, growth, family history.
  • Physical exam: location, timing, intensity.
  • ECG and Chest X-ray if needed.
  • Echocardiography is diagnostic.

Arrhythmias in Children

Arrhythmia is any abnormality in heart rhythm due to problems in impulse formation or conduction.

  • May be benign or life-threatening.
  • Present with palpitations, syncope, or shock.

Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT)

  • Most common arrhythmia in children.
  • HR often >220/min in infants.
  • Symptoms: poor feeding, irritability, pallor.
  • ECG: narrow QRS tachycardia.
  • Treat: vagal maneuvers → adenosine.

Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

  • Wide QRS tachycardia.
  • Often associated with heart disease.
  • May cause hypotension or collapse.
  • Treat: cardioversion if unstable, antiarrhythmics.

Bradyarrhythmias & Heart Block

  • Sinus bradycardia: often benign.
  • Complete heart block: very low HR.
  • May present with syncope or heart failure.
  • Pacemaker indicated in symptomatic cases.

Clinical Presentation of Arrhythmias

  • Palpitations.
  • Dizziness or syncope.
  • Poor feeding, lethargy in infants.
  • Shock in severe cases.

Investigations

  • 12-lead ECG.
  • Holter monitoring.
  • Event recorder.
  • Echocardiography to exclude structural disease.
  • Electrolytes and drug levels if indicated.

Management Principles

  • Assess hemodynamic stability.
  • Acute treatment according to rhythm.
  • Correct underlying causes.
  • Long-term: antiarrhythmics or ablation.
  • Parental education.

Prognosis

  • Innocent murmurs: excellent, no treatment needed.
  • SVT: good with proper management.
  • Serious arrhythmias: depend on cause and response.
