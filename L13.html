Salicylate Toxicity Summary

Comprehensive guide to salicylate toxicity and management

General Information

Salicylates are weak acids, widely used (over 200 compounds).

Common Forms

Acetylsalicylic Acid (ASA)

Commonly known as aspirin, used for pain relief and anti-inflammatory purposes.

Methyl Salicylate

Very toxic topically, found in liniments and topical pain relief products.

Salicylic Acid

Primarily used for wart removal and skin exfoliation.

Warning: Methyl salicylate is highly toxic when applied topically, especially in large amounts.

Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Rapid absorption in the jejunum. Delayed in toxic doses due to pylorospasm and concretions.

Distribution

Low volume of distribution (0.2–0.5 L/kg). Crosses blood-brain barrier more easily in acidic pH.

Metabolism

Liver conjugation. Half-life: 3–5h (therapeutic), 15h (toxic).

Excretion

Mainly renal excretion.

Pathophysiology

Mechanism of Toxicity

CNS

Stimulation → depression → cerebral edema

Ear

Tinnitus, hearing loss

Respiratory

Hyperpnea → failure, pulmonary edema

CVS

Hypotension

GIT

Nausea/vomiting, hemorrhage, hepatitis-like syndrome

Renal

Impairment due to decreased prostaglandins

Blood

Decreased coagulation factors (II, V, VII, IX), platelet dysfunction

Metabolic

Uncoupling oxidative phosphorylation, hyper→hypoglycemia, dehydration, hypokalemia

Acid-Base Disturbance

Respiratory alkalosis + metabolic acidosis

Clinical Picture

Earliest Symptoms

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Sweating
  • Tinnitus

System-Based Manifestations

GIT/Liver

  • Nausea/vomiting
  • Bleeding
  • Rye's syndrome (hypoglycemia, elevated liver enzymes)

CVS

  • Hypotension
  • Pulmonary edema

CNS

  • Agitation, headache
  • Confusion, coma

Respiratory

  • Hyperpnea
  • Pulmonary edema

Blood

  • Prolonged PT
  • Platelet dysfunction

Metabolic

  • Hyper/hypoglycemia
  • Hypokalemia
  • Dehydration

Allergy

  • Asthma
  • Urticaria
  • Anaphylaxis

Renal

  • Tubular necrosis
  • Uremia

Toxicity Information

Fatal Dose

~300 mg/kg (~60–70 tablets)

Fatal Period

Within 24 hours

Cause of Death

Early: respiratory failure, arrhythmia

Late: renal failure, hemorrhage

Mode

Mainly suicidal

Accidental (children)

Homicidal (rare)

Acid-Base Disturbances

Respiratory alkalosis (hyperventilation) → metabolic acidosis (↑ organic acids, renal impairment, ketoacidosis).

Children present earlier with metabolic acidosis.

Severe dose may cause respiratory acidosis (hypoventilation).

Treatment

Emergency Management

ABC (Airway, Breathing, Circulation)

Decontamination

  • Emesis
  • Gastric lavage
  • Activated charcoal (multiple doses)
  • Whole bowel irrigation (for concretions)

Antidote

No specific antidote available

Enhance Elimination

  • Alkaline diuresis (ion trapping with NaHCO₃, fluids, K+)
  • Hemodialysis (indicated when serum >100 mg/dl, CNS symptoms present, renal failure, pulmonary edema, or severe acid-base/electrolyte disturbance)

Supportive Care

  • Cooling for fever
  • Fluid replacement
  • Vitamin K for coagulation abnormalities
  • Transfusion if needed
  • Benzodiazepines for seizures
  • Antihistamines for hypersensitivity reactions

Investigations

  • Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) analysis
  • Electrolytes
  • Complete Blood Count (CBC)
  • Coagulation profile
  • Renal and liver function tests
  • Urine analysis
  • Serum salicylate levels: 30–50 mg/dl (mild), 50–80 (moderate), >80 (severe)
  • X-ray: pulmonary edema, concretions in abdomen

Differential Diagnosis

  • High anion gap acidosis: DKA, liver/kidney failure, alcoholic ketoacidosis
  • CNS infection or trauma
  • CO or cyanide poisoning
