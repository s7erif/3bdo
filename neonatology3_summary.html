Neonatal Jaundice, Kernicterus & Birth Injuries – Modern Summary

Neonatal Jaundice

  • Yellow discoloration due to elevated serum bilirubin.
  • Visible when bilirubin ≈ ≥7 mg/dL.
  • Normal serum bilirubin ≈ 1 mg/dL.
  • Common in newborns due to immature liver handling.

Bilirubin Metabolism

  • Produced from RBC breakdown → unconjugated bilirubin.
  • Transported bound to albumin.
  • Hepatic uptake → conjugation by glucuronyl transferase.
  • Excreted in bile to intestine.
  • Unbound unconjugated bilirubin can cross BBB → kernicterus.

Types of Hyperbilirubinemia

  • Unconjugated: physiologic, hemolysis, enzyme defects.
  • Conjugated: cholestasis, hepatitis, biliary obstruction.
  • Mixed: liver cell diseases, sepsis.

Physiological Jaundice

  • Most common cause (≈60% term, 80% preterm).
  • Onset: day 2–3; peak <15 mg/dL in term.
  • Rate of rise <5 mg/dL/day.
  • Always unconjugated.
  • Resolves by 7–10 days (term).

Pathological Jaundice

  • Onset in first 24 h of life.
  • Rise >5 mg/dL/day.
  • Peak >15 mg/dL or persists >2 weeks.
  • May be conjugated or unconjugated.
  • Requires evaluation & treatment.

Causes by Age of Onset

  • <24 h: Rh/ABO incompatibility, sepsis, cephalhematoma.
  • Day 2–3: Physiologic jaundice, Crigler–Najjar.
  • After 1st week: Sepsis, hepatitis, biliary atresia, breast milk jaundice, hypothyroidism, G6PD.
  • Persistent >2 weeks: Hepatitis, biliary atresia, TORCH, hypothyroidism.

Breast Jaundice

  • Breastfeeding jaundice: early, due to poor intake → dehydration & ↑ enterohepatic circulation.
  • Breast milk jaundice: late (2–3rd week), milk inhibits conjugation.
  • Bilirubin may reach 10–30 mg/dL.
  • Temporarily stop feeding for 48 h → bilirubin falls.

Conjugated Hyperbilirubinemia

  • Direct bilirubin >1.5 mg/dL.
  • Features: pale stools, dark urine, hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Causes: neonatal hepatitis, biliary atresia, choledochal cyst.
  • Always pathological → urgent evaluation.

Kernicterus (BIND)

  • Bilirubin-induced neurologic dysfunction.
  • Unconjugated bilirubin deposits in basal ganglia.
  • Risk factors: prematurity, hemolysis, sepsis, acidosis.
  • Features: lethargy, hypotonia → seizures, opisthotonus.
  • Prevention by early treatment of jaundice.

Hemolytic Disease of Newborn

  • Due to Rh or ABO incompatibility.
  • Maternal IgG antibodies destroy fetal RBCs.
  • Causes anemia, jaundice, may lead to hydrops fetalis.
  • Diagnosis: blood groups, Coombs test.
  • Prevention: Anti-D immunoglobulin to Rh− mother.
