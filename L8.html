Cocaine and Cannabis

Comprehensive Medical Summary

Cocaine Overview

Source & Characteristics

  • Alkaloid from Erythroxylon coca leaves
  • Bitter white crystalline powder
  • Isolated in 1880
  • Coca-Cola originally contained cocaine

Uses

  • Medical: historically for digestive disorders, now local anesthetic and antiarrhythmic
  • Non-medical: doping in sports, appetite suppression

Toxicokinetics

  • Rapid absorption via mucosa and skin
  • Metabolized in liver, excreted by kidneys
  • Routes: snuffing, smoking (crack), IV

Toxicodynamics

  • Sympathomimetic: increases release and decreases reuptake of NE, E, dopamine, serotonin
  • Local anesthesia: blocks sodium channels

Mode of Poisoning & Fatality

  • Usually accidental (overdose, addicts, body packers)
  • Fatal dose: ~100 mg IV or ~1 g orally
  • Fatal period: 2–8 hours
  • Cause of death: arrhythmia, hyperthermia, respiratory failure, collapse

Clinical Picture

  • Stimulation: euphoria, hallucinations, headache, convulsions, dilated pupils, hypertension, tachycardia, tachypnea, hyperthermia
  • Depression: coma, respiratory failure, circulatory collapse
  • Other: chest pain (MI), tachyarrhythmia, stroke, hemorrhage

Investigations

  • CBC, ECG, renal function, ABG, abdominal X-ray (for body packers)
  • Urine metabolites detectable for 2–3 days

Treatment

  • Emergency: airway, ventilation, monitoring
  • Decontamination: emesis, gastric lavage, MDAC, WBI
  • No specific antidote
  • Symptomatic: diazepam for seizures, propranolol/phenytoin for arrhythmias

Cannabis Overview

Source & Characteristics

  • Derived from Cannabis sativa plant (subtypes indica, Americana, synthetic forms)
  • Most widely used recreational drug
  • High morbidity, low mortality
  • Active principle: THC (main), cannabinol, cannabidiol
  • Forms: hashish (tops), bango (leaves/stems)

Uses

  • Medical: anticonvulsant, antiemetic, antispasmodic, analgesic in cancer, appetite stimulant in AIDS
  • Non-medical: addiction, manufacture of ropes/paints

Toxicokinetics

  • Rapid absorption by inhalation (3–4x oral)
  • Peak: minutes (inhaled), 2h (oral)
  • Metabolized by liver/lungs, stored in fat
  • Excreted in stool (65–80%) and urine (20–35%)
  • Urine detection: 5–8 weeks (addicts), 5–8 days (occasional users)

Toxicodynamics

  • CNS: THC receptors concentrated in cortex, cerebellum, basal ganglia → morbidity
  • Low in brainstem → low mortality
  • Cardiac: stimulates sympathetic, inhibits parasympathetic tone

Mode of Poisoning & Fatality

  • Accidental among addicts
  • Homicidal/suicidal not reported
  • Fatal dose not reported (experimental: 25 g/kg)
  • Cause of death: respiratory depression, MI

Clinical Picture

  • Acute: Psychic - euphoria, dream-like, hallucinations, memory loss, delusions, agitation, psychosis
  • CVS: tachycardia, hypertension, rare MI/arrhythmia
  • Others: mydriasis, tremors, tinnitus, hyperreflexia, confusion, rare coma, asthma exacerbation
  • Chronic: habituation, amotivational syndrome, osteoporosis, sterility
  • Maternal use: low birth weight, fetal anomalies (brain, cardiac, GI)

Investigations

  • General: CBC, LFT, RFT, ABG, ECG
  • Specific: TLC (scarlet color THC, violet cannabinol), Beam's test, urine rapid kits

Treatment

  • Emergency: airway, oxygen, monitor vitals
  • Decontamination: emesis, gastric lavage, charcoal
  • No specific antidote
  • Symptomatic: reassurance, oxygenation, benzodiazepines

Key Comparisons

Cocaine

  • Strong psychic dependence, mild physical dependence
  • Fatal dose: ~100 mg IV or ~1 g orally
  • Urine detection: 2-3 days
  • Higher mortality risk

Cannabis

  • High morbidity, low mortality
  • No reported fatal dose in humans
  • Urine detection: 5-8 weeks (addicts)
  • Widely used recreationally
