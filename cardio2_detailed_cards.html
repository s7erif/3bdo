Congenital Heart Diseases: Acyanotic & Cyanotic

Overview

Cardio 2 focuses on congenital heart diseases (CHD), their classification into acyanotic and cyanotic lesions, pathophysiology, clinical presentation, and basic management principles.

Classification of CHD

  • Acyanotic (L→R shunts): ASD, VSD, PDA, AV canal.
  • Obstructive: Coarctation, aortic/pulmonary stenosis.
  • Cyanotic (R→L shunts): TOF, TGA, Tricuspid atresia, TAPVC.

Pathophysiology

  • L→R shunt → pulmonary overcirculation → heart failure.
  • R→L shunt → systemic desaturation → cyanosis.
  • Chronic shunts → pulmonary hypertension.
  • Late: Eisenmenger syndrome (reversal of shunt).

Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)

  • Defect in interatrial septum.
  • Often asymptomatic in infancy.
  • Features: recurrent infections, exercise intolerance.
  • Signs: fixed wide split S2, systolic murmur.
  • Treatment: device or surgical closure if significant.

Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)

  • Most common CHD.
  • Small: loud murmur, minimal symptoms.
  • Large: CHF, failure to thrive.
  • Signs: pansystolic murmur at LLSB.
  • Many close spontaneously; surgery if persistent.

Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)

  • Persistent connection between aorta & pulmonary artery.
  • Common in preterm infants.
  • Continuous machinery murmur.
  • Wide pulse pressure.
  • Treatment: indomethacin/ibuprofen or closure.

Cyanotic CHD: Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)

  • Four components: VSD, RVOT obstruction, overriding aorta, RVH.
  • Most common cyanotic CHD after infancy.
  • Cyanosis, clubbing, squatting, spells.
  • Harsh systolic murmur.
  • Definitive surgical repair.

Hypercyanotic (Tet) Spells

  • Sudden worsening of cyanosis & dyspnea.
  • Triggered by crying or feeding.
  • Management: knee-chest position, oxygen, morphine.
  • Beta-blockers for prevention.

Investigations in CHD

  • Chest X-ray: heart size, pulmonary flow.
  • ECG: chamber hypertrophy.
  • Echocardiography: diagnostic gold standard.
  • Pulse oximetry for screening.

Management Principles

  • Stabilize: oxygen, treat CHF.
  • Prevent complications: infections, anemia.
  • Timely referral for intervention.
  • Definitive surgical or catheter-based correction.

Complications

  • Heart failure.
  • Failure to thrive.
  • Arrhythmias.
  • Pulmonary hypertension.
  • Infective endocarditis.
