Rheumatic Fever, Cardiomyopathies & Pediatric Hypertension

Overview

Cardio 4 covers important acquired heart diseases in children including acute rheumatic fever (ARF), rheumatic heart disease (RHD), cardiomyopathies, infective endocarditis, and hypertension.

Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF)

ARF is an autoimmune inflammatory disease that follows infection with Group A β-hemolytic streptococcus.

  • Affects heart, joints, CNS, skin.
  • Common in school-age children.
  • Leads to chronic rheumatic heart disease.

Jones Criteria

Major

  • Carditis
  • Polyarthritis
  • Chorea
  • Erythema marginatum
  • Subcutaneous nodules

Minor

  • Fever
  • Arthralgia
  • Elevated ESR/CRP
  • Prolonged PR interval

Carditis in ARF

  • Pancarditis: endocardium, myocardium, pericardium.
  • Mitral valve most commonly affected.
  • Features: tachycardia, murmurs, heart failure.
  • Echocardiography confirms diagnosis.

Management of ARF

  • Eradicate streptococcus: benzathine penicillin.
  • Anti-inflammatory: aspirin or steroids.
  • Treat heart failure if present.
  • Bed rest during acute phase.

Secondary Prophylaxis

  • Long-term benzathine penicillin IM every 3–4 weeks.
  • Prevents recurrence and progression to RHD.
  • Duration depends on cardiac involvement.

Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD)

  • Chronic valvular damage following ARF.
  • Most common: mitral stenosis/regurgitation.
  • May lead to heart failure, arrhythmias, emboli.
  • Management: medical therapy or valve surgery.

Cardiomyopathies

  • Dilated: systolic dysfunction, most common.
  • Hypertrophic: thick LV walls, may cause sudden death.
  • Restrictive: impaired filling.
  • Causes: genetic, viral, metabolic.

Clinical Features of Cardiomyopathy

  • Heart failure symptoms.
  • Arrhythmias.
  • Syncope.
  • Sudden cardiac death (especially HCM).

Management of Cardiomyopathy

  • Heart failure therapy.
  • Antiarrhythmic drugs if needed.
  • ICD in high-risk HCM.
  • Heart transplantation in severe cases.

Infective Endocarditis

  • Infection of endocardial surface.
  • Risk: CHD, prosthetic valves.
  • Features: fever, murmur, embolic signs.
  • Diagnosis: blood cultures, echo.
  • Treatment: prolonged IV antibiotics.

Pediatric Hypertension

  • BP ≥95th percentile for age, sex, height.
  • Secondary causes common: renal, coarctation.
  • Symptoms: often asymptomatic, headache.
  • Evaluate with BP measurements & investigations.

Management of Hypertension

  • Lifestyle: diet, weight control, exercise.
  • Treat underlying cause.
  • Drugs: ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, CCBs.
  • Urgent management in hypertensive emergencies.
