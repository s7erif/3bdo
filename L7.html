Organophosphorus Insecticides (OPI)

Mechanism, Toxicity, and Treatment

By: Dr. Mostafa M Asem
Lecturer of forensic medicine and toxicology

Mechanism of Toxicity

  • Organophosphate compounds bind to cholinesterase transforming them into enzymatically inert proteins, thus acting as irreversible cholinesterase inhibitors
  • Inhibition of cholinesterase activity leads to the accumulation of acetylcholine at synapses, causing overstimulation and subsequent disruption of transmission in both the central and peripheral nervous systems
  • Inhibition of the enzyme initially by ionic bonding, then the enzyme is progressively phosphorylated by covalent bond which may take 24-48h and called "aging" and this period is called "critical interval" during this period, the administration of antidote is effective in reversing this process
  • Once aging occurs, antidote is not effective and enzyme re-synthesis is the only way to restore the enzyme activity that needs several weeks (120 days)

Key Points

The "critical interval" of 24-48 hours is the window for effective antidote administration before aging occurs.

Mode of Poisoning

Modes of Poisoning

  • Suicidal: Very common in Egyptian population
  • Accidental: Common in agriculture or home
  • Homicidal: Not common when the insecticide is mixed with food or drinks

Fatal Dose & Period

  • Fatal dose: Usually ranges from 0.5 - 300mg/kg
  • Fatal period: 2-12h

Cause of Death

Respiratory failure due to:

  • Central: depression of respiratory center
  • Peripheral:
    • Muscarinic: increased bronchial secretions, bronchospasm
    • Nicotinic: Respiratory muscle paralysis

Clinical Presentation

Central (3CD)
  • Confusion
  • Convulsion
  • Coma
  • Depression of respiratory center
Muscarinic (DUMBBLES)
  • Diarrhea
  • Urinary incontinence
  • Miosis
  • Bronchospasm
  • Bronchorrhea
  • Emesis
  • Salivation, Lacrimation
Nicotinic (MATCH)
  • Muscle weakness & fasciculations
  • Adrenal medulla activity increased
  • Tachycardia
  • Cramping of skeletal muscles
  • Hypertension
Other Findings
  • Chemical pneumonitis if a product containing a hydrocarbon solvent is aspirated into the lungs
  • Garlic like odour in the breath/vomit/clothes

Intermediate Syndrome

Timing

Usually presents 12 to 96 hours after exposure

Manifestations

Early: Tremors and Pharyngeal weakness (pooling of secretions in pharynx)

Later:

  • Inability to flex neck
  • Deep tendon jerks are lost
  • Cranial neuropathies
  • Proximal muscle weakness
  • Respiratory muscle paralysis

Pathophysiology

May be combined pre- and post-synaptic dysfunction of neuromuscular transmission

Associated Compounds & Treatment

More common with methyl parathion and dimethoate. Treatment is mainly supportive.

Delayed Peripheral Neuropathy

Timing

Occurs within 1-2 weeks. It may be irreversible.

Clinical Course

The neuropathy begins with paresthesia and pain or cramping in the calves followed by ataxia, weakness and toe drop.

The disease may progress for 2-3 months and muscle wasting occurs.

Pathophysiology

Damage to the afferent fibers of peripheral and central nerves and associated with inhibition of "neuropathy target esterase" (NTE)

Treatment

Treatment is difficult & no role of antidote

Investigations

General Laboratory Investigations

  • CBC: Neutrophil leukocytosis
  • Electrolytes: Hypokalemia
  • Blood glucose level: Hyperglycemia
  • Urine analysis: Proteinuria and Glycosuria
  • Serum amylase: Elevated serum amylase 2ry to pancreatic injury by parasympathetic overstimulation

Toxin Specific Investigations

  • Assay of blood and urine for specific agents (not widely available)
  • Measurement of acetylcholine esterase level:
    • True acetyl cholinesterase is more specific
    • Pseudocholinesterase is more sensitive of exposure (25% depression in its level is an indicator of poisoning)
    • True cholinesterase takes 90-120 days to return to normal values
    • Pseudocholinesterase takes days to weeks to return to normal

Cholinesterase Depression

Pseudocholinesterase Depression
  • Parenchymal liver diseases (hepatitis, cirrhosis, cancer...)
  • Malnutrition
  • Early pregnancy, oral contraception
  • Acute infections
  • Chronic alcoholism
  • Cocaine and metoclopramide
  • Genetically in 3% of normal persons (vulnerable to the action of the muscle-paralyzing drug succinylcholine)
True Cholinesterase Depression
  • Hemolytic anemia

Treatment Overview

  1. Preventive measures
  2. 1st aid measures
  3. Decontamination
    • GIT: emesis, gastric wash, cathartics. AC (Controverse)
    • Dermal: wash by water and soap
  4. Antidotes
  5. Supportive and symptomatic treatment

Atropine Therapy

Mechanism & Effects

  • It treats both muscarinic and central effects
  • It has no role on nicotinic sites (NMJ & adrenal medulla)

Atropine Test

If you are not sure if the patient has consumed OP, inject 1 mg IV atropine. If pulse rate goes up by 25 per minute or skin flushing develops, patient has mild or no toxicity.

Bolus Dose Administration

Defined as (5 mg of atropine every 15 minutes) followed by maintenance using reduced doses or increasing the time duration in between the doses till full atropinization.

Then slowly withdrawal.

Oximes (Specific Antidotes)

Examples

Pralidoxime (protopam), Obidoxime (toxogonin)

Mechanism of Action

Oximes attach to the free anionic site of the enzyme ChE. The oxime end then reacts with the phosphorus atom of OP attached at the esteratic site of the enzyme. This formed oxime phosphate complex diffuses away leaving the enzyme intact (Reactivated ChE).

Timing is Critical

It must be given in the first 24-48h post-exposure (critical period) {before aging of the enzyme}.

Dose

  • Bolus dose: 250 mg over 30 minutes
  • Maintenance dose: continuous infusion of 750 mg/24 hour

A fast infusion can cause vomiting, hypertension, cardiac arrhythmia or cardiac arrest.

OPI vs Carbamates

Feature Organophosphate Carbamates
Mechanism of action Irreversible inhibition of acetylcholine choline estrase (damage of enzyme). Dissociate slowly. Reversible inhibition. Dissociate readily.
Kinetics Penetrate CNS. Longer duration. Poorly penetrate CNS. Shorter duration.
Symptoms & signs Late onset. Severe & prolonged. Prominent CNS effects. Quick onset. Mild & short. Less prominent.
Diagnosis Serum & RBCs A.ch.E levels are more reliable. Depend on lab findings. Less reliable. Depends on clinical findings.
Treatment Atropine, Pralidoxime Atropine, No Pralidoxime

Carbamate Insecticides

Introduction

They are group of insecticides derived from carbamic acid.

Mechanism of Toxicity

Like organophosphorus compounds, they are cholinesterase inhibitors by carbamylation of acetyl cholinesterase enzyme leading to accumulation of acetylcholine at cholinergic nerve endings.

However, the carbamate insecticides differ from organophosphates in being "reversible inhibitors" of cholinesterase thus they are of more benign toxicity than organophosphate and of short duration of action.

Also, they poorly penetrate CNS thus, central effects are less prominent.
