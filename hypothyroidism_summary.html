Hypothyroidism

Modern concise summary for medical students

Overview

  • Hypothyroidism: deficiency of thyroid hormone production.
  • Leads to slowing of metabolic processes.
  • In children, early detection is critical to prevent brain damage.

Functions of Thyroid Hormone

  • Brain growth, neurogenesis, and myelination.
  • Growth of skeleton and organs.
  • Increases heart rate and cardiac output.
  • Stimulates metabolism and oxygen consumption.
  • Enhances GI motility and RBC synthesis.

Classification

  • By onset: Congenital or Acquired.
  • By site:
  • Primary: thyroid gland defect (↑TSH, ↓T4/T3).
  • Secondary/Central: pituitary or hypothalamic (↓TSH, ↓T4/T3).

Congenital Hypothyroidism

  • Incidence ≈ 1 in 4000 births; more common in girls.
  • Main cause: thyroid dysgenesis (~85%).
  • Others: hormone synthesis defects, iodine deficiency, rare TSH deficiency.
  • Early signs: prolonged jaundice, hypotonia, constipation, poor feeding.
  • Late (cretinism): mental retardation, coarse features, short stature.

Screening & Diagnosis

  • Neonatal heel-prick screening at 3–7 days.
  • TSH > 40 μIU/ml suggests hypothyroidism.
  • Low T4 confirms diagnosis.
  • Imaging: ultrasound or radioisotope scan.
  • X-ray knee: delayed ossification.

Treatment (Congenital)

  • Immediate levothyroxine therapy.
  • Dose: 10–15 μg/kg/day in neonates.
  • Goal: normalize TSH and keep T4 in upper normal range.
  • Regular monitoring of growth and development.

Acquired Hypothyroidism

  • More common in school-age children & adolescents.
  • Girls affected more than boys.
  • Common cause: Hashimoto thyroiditis.
  • Others: iodine deficiency, drugs, post-irradiation.
  • Features: poor growth, fatigue, cold intolerance, dry skin, goiter.

Treatment & Prognosis

  • Levothyroxine 25–200 μg/day for acquired cases.
  • Monitor TFTs every 4–6 months.
  • Early treatment (<3 months) → normal growth & intelligence.
  • Delayed treatment → risk of permanent brain damage.
