Bacterial Infections

Approach to Fever & Common Pediatric Bacterial Diseases

Overview

Bacterial infections are important causes of morbidity and mortality in children. Early recognition, proper evaluation, and timely antibiotic therapy are essential to prevent serious complications.

Fever in Children

  • Definition: >37.8°C oral or >38°C rectal.
  • Low: 38–38.4°C, Moderate: 38.5–39.4°C.
  • High: 39.5–41°C, Hyperpyrexia: >41°C.
  • Most common cause is viral, but bacterial must be excluded.

Types of Febrile Illness

  • Short febrile illness: <7 days.
  • Prolonged fever: >10–14 days.
  • With focus (localized infection) or without focus.

Viremia vs Bacteremia

  • Viremia: mild fever, fair general condition.
  • Normal CBC or mild leukocytosis.
  • CRP/ESR usually normal.
  • Treatment: antipyretics.
  • Bacteremia: high fever, toxic child.
  • Leukocytosis or leukopenia, ↑CRP/ESR.
  • Treatment: broad-spectrum antibiotics.

Red Flag Signs

  • High fever in young infants.
  • Pallor, mottling, or cyanosis.
  • Altered consciousness, seizures, bulging fontanelle.
  • Severe respiratory distress.
  • Bile-stained vomiting.

Septicemia (Sepsis)

Life‑threatening systemic response to infection causing organ dysfunction.

  • Gram negative: meningococcus, E. coli, Klebsiella.
  • Gram positive: Staph. aureus, Strep. pneumoniae.
  • Features: high fever, poor feeding, lethargy, shock.
  • Purpuric rash in meningococcal sepsis.

Septic Workup

  • CBC with differential.
  • CRP, ESR.
  • Blood & urine cultures.
  • CSF if meningitis suspected.
  • Chest X‑ray if respiratory signs.

Management of Sepsis

  • Immediate IV broad‑spectrum antibiotics.
  • Fluid resuscitation.
  • Inotropes if shock.
  • Oxygen and supportive care.
  • ICU care if unstable.

Streptococcal Pharyngitis

  • High fever, sore throat, vomiting.
  • Tonsillar exudate, cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Treatment: penicillin for 10 days.
  • Prevents rheumatic fever.

Scarlet Fever

  • Strep infection with erythrogenic toxin.
  • Strawberry tongue.
  • Sandpaper rash with Pastia lines.
  • Desquamation during recovery.

Diphtheria

  • Gray adherent membrane on tonsils/pharynx.
  • Bull neck due to lymphadenitis.
  • Toxin causes myocarditis & neuropathy.
  • Treatment: antitoxin + antibiotics.
  • Prevention: DPT vaccine.

Pertussis (Whooping Cough)

  • Catarrhal → paroxysmal → convalescent stages.
  • Paroxysmal cough with whoop & post‑tussive vomiting.
  • Leukocytosis with lymphocytosis.
  • Treatment: macrolides.
  • Prevention: DPT vaccine.

Enteric (Typhoid) Fever

  • Cause: Salmonella typhi.
  • Step‑ladder fever, abdominal pain, hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Rose spots on trunk.
  • Diagnosis: blood culture, Widal.
  • Treatment: ceftriaxone, cefixime, azithro.

Complications

  • Shock and DIC.
  • Meningitis, pneumonia.
  • Osteomyelitis, septic arthritis.
  • Intestinal hemorrhage or perforation in typhoid.

Prevention

  • Routine immunization (DPT, Hib, pneumococcal).
  • Hand hygiene and sanitation.
  • Early treatment of cases.
  • Contact prophylaxis when indicated.
