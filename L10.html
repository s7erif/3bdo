Atropine (Belladonna Alkaloids)

Complete Pharmacological and Toxicological Summary

Comprehensive information on sources, pharmacology, mechanism of action, uses, and toxicology of atropine and related belladonna alkaloids.

Source

  • Alkaloids: Atropine, Hyoscyamine, Hyoscine
  • Found in Atropa belladonna, Hyoscyamus muticus, and Datura plants
  • Common in Egypt
  • Highest concentration in roots and seeds

Pharmacology

  • Lipophilic - absorbed by all routes
  • Crosses the blood-brain barrier (BBB)
  • Metabolism: 70% in liver
  • Excretion: 30% by kidney

Mechanism of Action

  • Anticholinergic: competitive inhibition of acetylcholine
  • Acts at both nicotinic & muscarinic receptors
  • Works both centrally & peripherally
  • Effects: ↓ all body secretions (except milk, bile, pancreatic)
  • Bronchodilatation, mydriasis, tachycardia, constipation
  • Urinary retention, atropine flush & fever

Pathophysiology

Atropine & Hyoscyamine:

  • Central: CNS stimulation → later depression (exhaustion)
  • Peripheral: antimuscarinic blockade
  • Dry eye, dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, tachycardia

Hyoscine:

  • Central: CNS depression directly
  • Peripheral: milder effects

Therapeutic Uses

Atropine:

  • Pre-anesthetic (↓ secretions, prevent laryngospasm)
  • Peptic ulcer (↓ secretion, antispasmodic)
  • Ophthalmology (fundus exam)
  • Antidote: organophosphorus, aconite, β-blockers, CCBs, digitalis

Hyoscine:

  • Pre-anesthetic (better than atropine)
  • Motion sickness, parkinsonism, mania
  • Obstetric analgesia, enuresis, dysmenorrhea

Toxicology

  • Mode: Accidental overdose, 'Manzool' mixture, rarely suicidal/homicidal
  • Fatal dose: Atropine/Hyoscyamine 120 mg, Hyoscine 30 mg
  • Fatal dose: >100 datura seeds
  • Fatal period: 12–24h
  • Cause of death: respiratory center depression → respiratory failure
  • Arrhythmias, hyperthermia

Clinical Picture (Atropinism)

Central Stimulation:

  • Agitation, hallucinations
  • Purposeless movements
  • Convulsions

Central Depression:

  • Drowsiness, coma
  • Slow/irregular breathing
  • Asphyxia

Peripheral Effects:

  • Dry hot red skin (flush)
  • Dry eyes/mouth
  • Dilated fixed pupils
  • Tachycardia
  • Constipation, paralytic ileus
  • Urine retention
  • Atropine fever

Investigations

  • Routine: CBC, electrolytes, renal function
  • ECG (sinus tachycardia)
  • No specific test for atropine

Differential Diagnosis

  • Other anticholinergics (antihistamines, TCA)
  • Encephalitis delirium
  • Alcohol intoxication (cold, moist skin vs atropine hot, dry)
  • Barbiturate coma (cyanotic, clammy vs atropine flushed, hot, dry)

Management

  • Emergency: ABC (Airway, Breathing, Circulation)
  • Decontamination: Gastric lavage (up to 18–24h), potassium permanganate
  • Specific antidotes:
    • Pilocarpine: reverses peripheral effects only (10 mg IV)
    • Physostigmine: crosses BBB, reverses central & peripheral symptoms, short-acting (1–4 mg IV in adults)
  • Use antidotes only when severe CNS/CVS symptoms present
  • Supportive: Diazepam (convulsions), propranolol/phenytoin (arrhythmias), cooling (fever), coma care

Key Points to Remember

  • Atropine is a competitive inhibitor of acetylcholine at both muscarinic and nicotinic receptors
  • It decreases all body secretions except milk, bile, and pancreatic secretions
  • Hyoscine causes direct CNS depression, unlike atropine which causes stimulation followed by depression
  • Physostigmine is the specific antidote that can cross the blood-brain barrier
  • The classic presentation of atropine poisoning includes dry, hot, red skin; dilated pupils; and tachycardia
